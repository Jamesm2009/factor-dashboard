How to Use This Dashboard

This tool scores S&P 500 sector ETFs and US equity style/factor ETFs on two dimensions — Absolute Score and Relative Score (vs SPY) — to help identify which areas of the market are overbought or oversold, both in isolation and relative to the S&P 500.

What the Scores Mean

Each score maps to a range of −1.00 (extremely oversold) to +1.00 (extremely overbought), based on where the ETF's price sits within its trailing 52-week range.

Score RangeReadingInterpretation
−1.00 to −0.60Extremely OversoldDeep in the lower end of 52-week range — potential value opportunity
−0.60 to −0.20OversoldBelow midpoint — could be a good time to increase holdings
−0.20 to +0.20Fair ValueNear the middle of its range — no strong signal
+0.20 to +0.60OverboughtAbove midpoint — could be a good time to trim
+0.60 to +1.00Extremely OverboughtNear the top of 52-week range — extended, be cautious

Reading the Scatter Chart

The chart plots Absolute Score on the X-axis and Relative Score on the Y-axis. This creates four quadrants:

↖ Top-Left: Oversold + Outperforming
Cheap but showing relative strength vs SPY. Often the most interesting quadrant for new positions.
↗ Top-Right: Overbought + Outperforming
Strong and expensive. Momentum is positive but be aware of potential mean reversion.
↙ Bottom-Left: Oversold + Underperforming
Weak and cheap. Could be contrarian value, or a falling knife — context matters.
↘ Bottom-Right: Overbought + Underperforming
Expensive but losing relative strength. Potential trim or rotation candidate.

Using the Tails

The tail feature is what makes this tool most actionable. When you select a tail length (4, 6, 8, 10, or 12 weeks), each checked ETF shows a trailing path of its weekly score positions. This reveals the direction of travel — not just where something is, but where it's heading.

Key insight: An ETF moving from the bottom-left toward the top-right is gaining both absolute and relative strength — that's a momentum signal. An ETF moving from top-right toward bottom-left is weakening on both fronts — potential distribution.

Seasonality Tab

Shows this year's performance so far (rebased to 0% at Jan 1) alongside the average seasonal path over the last 25 years. Click any line in the top chart to add its long-run seasonal average to the bottom chart — useful for spotting whether an ETF is running ahead of, or behind, its typical pattern for this time of year. Dashed vertical lines mark average month-end points so the two charts stay aligned.

Important: Seasonality shows historical averages, not predictions. Any single year can and does deviate from the average — treat it as context, not a signal on its own.

Practical Workflow

1. Start with the table. Sort by Relative Score to see which areas are most oversold or overbought vs the S&P 500. Green cells = oversold (opportunity), red cells = overbought (caution).

2. Check interesting ETFs. Select the ones you want to compare on the scatter chart. Use Select All to see the full picture, or pick a focused set.

3. Turn on tails. Start with 4 weeks for recent momentum, extend to 8–12 weeks for the bigger trend. Watch for ETFs crossing quadrant boundaries — that's where the signal is strongest.

4. Compare tabs. The Sectors tab shows where capital is flowing within the market. The Factors tab shows which investment styles are in or out of favor.

Important: Scores can remain extremely overbought or oversold for extended periods. These are positioning signals to inform allocation decisions — they are not precise timing signals. Patience and context are required.

Data & Refresh

Price data comes from Yahoo Finance. Scores are computed using a rolling 52-week lookback window. Data refreshes automatically after US market close (3:40 PM CT on trading days). Cached results load instantly on repeat visits. Seasonality data is refreshed separately (roughly monthly) since it requires a much larger historical download.
